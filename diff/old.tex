\chapter*{Abstract}
\markboth{Abstract}{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

Methane InfraRed Absorption Gas Experiment (MIRAGE) aims to measure stratospheric methane concentrations using an off-the-shelf Non-Dispersive InfraRed (NDIR) sensor. Current measurement techniques rely on laser-based setups, requiring fine adjustment while having a high cost. While these systems provide high resolution, they prevent frequent and cost-effective measurements. MIRAGE will demonstrate that NDIR sensors can fill the niche of low-cost measurements with reasonable resolution.

This is done by pressurizing temperature-controlled ambient air before feeding it into the optical chamber of the NDIR sensor. With this setup it is expected to achieve an absolute concentration accuracy of $\mathrm{\Delta}c \leq 0.1$ ppm.

\chapter{Introduction}

\section{Scientific/Technical Background}

Gases in the stratosphere play a pivotal role in climate modelling due to their prolonged lifetime. Especially methane sources have been shown to play a big role in climate modelling \cite{stocker2013, saunois2017, nisbet2019, turner2019}, and the need of accurate measurements was reflected on in the National Research Council Decadal Survey for Earth Science \cite{national2019} and the summary report by the Carbon Climate Workshop in 2018 \cite{sellers2018}.

The concentration of atmospheric methane ranges from around 2 ppm to 0.7 ppm at 0 km and 30 km altitude, respectively. Because of the small concentrations, accurate optically complex systems like \ac{tdlas} \cite{joly2020} or \ac{ftir} \cite{runge2023} are widely employed. \ac{tdlas} systems send a laser beam of varying frequency close to the excitation energy of the target molecule through a gas sample and calculate the target molecule's concentration based on the measured absorption via the Beer-Lambert law. \ac{ftir} works by sending a light beam containing multiple frequencies into the gas sample and recording the total absorption. The frequencies are then changed and the process repeated. This is often done via a Michelsen interferometer. Both techniques enable great accuracy but require high precision in the complex optical setups, meaning that those systems are expensive and inaccessible.

During recent years, \ac{ndir} sensors have advanced as a cost-effective and simple alternative to laser-based devices commonly employed for methane sensing. NDIR spectroscopy works by passing a spectrum of \ac{ir} light through a gas sample where each gas absorbs the light at a unique wavelength. An optical filter selects the wavelengths of the gas of interest. The intensity of the transmitted light at the relevant wavelength can then be measured, and thus the change in intensity is known. The concentration c of the gas can then be calculated using the Beer-Lambert law

\begin{equation}
c = \log_{10}\left( \frac{I_{0}}{I} \right) \times {(\varepsilon l)}^{- 1},
\end{equation}

where $\varepsilon$ is the molar absorption coefficient, specific to the gas species, $l$ is the path length between light source and detector and $I_{0}$ and $I$ are the initial intensity of light and the intensity at the detector. $I_{0}$ is determined either before the measurement or simultaneously using a second detector. A schematic is provided in Figure \ref{fig:ndir_principle}. Due to the simple setup, the overall cost of NDIR detectors is quite small with a range of tens to a few 100s of Euros.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=8cm]{Graphics/Figures/NDIR schematic.png}
\caption{Schematic of the non-dispersive infrared (NDIR) measurement principle. Source: \cite{gaynullin2025}}
\label{fig:ndir_principle}
\end{figure}

Since the pressure $p$ decreases exponentially with altitude, the concentration

\begin{equation}
c = \frac{n}{V}
\end{equation}

connected to the pressure by the ideal gas law

\begin{equation}
pV = nRT
\end{equation}

with the volume $V$, the ideal gas constant $R$ and the temperature $T$, decreases with about the same rate as the pressure. At the same time the relative molar fraction of methane molecules compared to other molecules in the air is decreasing, which means that there are very little methane molecules at the altitudes we can reach. This needs to be considered when working with a sensor offering lower resolution.

\section{Mission Statement}

As explained in Section 1.1 methane measurements are of great importance for climate modelling. However, systems with which methane measurements are taken, are oftentimes expensive and complex in setup. Therefore, our mission is to demonstrate a low-cost, low-complexity solution for measuring methane concentrations in the upper stratosphere. By using the novel K96 non-dispersive infrared (NDIR) sensor developed by Senseair (a Swedish company, specialised in state-of-the-art gas sensors) \cite{wastine2022} we will use off-the-shelf technology to provide an alternative to laser-based approaches.

\section{Experiment Concept}

The Non-Dispersive Infra-Red (NDIR) sensor detects light absorption due to the number of absorbing particles in the optical cavity within a narrow wavelength range. As mentioned in Section 1.1, this number is related to the concentration of the contributing gas species and their pressures and temperature. Thus, it is possible to increase the concentration of a gas species by increasing the pressure of the gas while maintaining the same relative composition and molar fractions of the sample.

Therefore, we use an NDIR sensor fed with pressurized ambient air under temperature-controlled conditions. The measurement resolution of the sensor is 0.1 ppm at ground level, i.e. at 1 bar. Because the uncertainty we aim for is less than 0.1 ppm, we need to pressurize the ambient air to 2 to 5 bar. Thus, we aim to use a pump to continuously pump ambient air into a pressurized chamber, in which the sensor is located. Air can leave the pressurized chamber through an electrical valve. This method of active air release can restrict the flow rate dynamically, adjusting to the pressure measured internally.

As the detector output can drift with temperature, we need to stabilize the temperature via a thermal control system. Additionally, we need information about the ambient pressure and temperature, to inform the thermal control system and the flow rate of the pumps. As water vapor is a major factor in the uncertainty of the sensor, we also need to monitor the inside and ideally outside humidity. These measurements can then be used to engage mitigation methods like stopping of the pumps and raising of the internal temperature to prevent condensation. Once the ambient humidity sensor is reporting safe levels, the internal temperature is to be set to normal operating conditions, and the pumps commence a full flushing cycle. This means that the pumps operate with the maximum flow rate for 2 minutes. Afterwards, the internal humidity levels are measured. If they are too high, another flushing cycle commences up to a total of 3 cycles, after which normal operations start again.

Processing of the different sensor outputs and engaging of appropriate actions will be done via a \ac{mcu} like an Arduino Nano.

\section{Experiment Setup}

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{Graphics/Figures/Mirage Block schematic.png}
    \caption{Experimental Setup}
    \label{fig:experimental_setup}
    
\end{figure}

The experiment will feature a NDIR sensor (K96), a pressurization system, a thermal system, ambient environment sensors and a processing unit. 
The pressurization system will include a pumping mechanism in order to increase the pressure and the amount of particles we can measure. The gas will be heated up and pumped into the measurement chamber with the K96 sensor inside together with temperature, pressure and humidity sensors. The gas will be exchanged continously to exchange the sample while maintaining the pressure of at least 2 bar. 
The K96 Sensor will communicate with the processing unit and the processing unit sends the measured digital data for storage to the SDHC card.
The pressure sensor,temperature sensor and humidity sensor communicate with the processing unit via digital interfaces (such as $I^2C$) to ensure that the pump and thermal control system are running with the right parameters to maintain room temperature conditions and a stable pressure between 2 and 5 bar. Unique addresses are used to identify each sensor's signal, allowing the processing unit to collect and organize data from all ambient sensors efficiently.

\section{Experiment Interfaces}


\subsection{Mechanical}
The experiment is mounted to the ESCARGO aluminium frame composed of 45 $\times$ 45~mm Bosch-type T-slot profiles used by the BEXUS gondola.

The experiment is housed in a rigid aluminium enclosure and is fixed to a structural aluminium base plate using vertical M6 bolts. The base plate forms the primary load-bearing interface of the experiment and ensures a rigid connection between the experiment enclosure and the gondola mounting structure.

The aluminium base plate is attached to the ESCARGO frame via horizontal M6 bolts engaging M6 T-nuts (or T-bolts) inserted into the ESCARGO T-slots. The horizontal bolt orientation allows direct fastening to the ESCARGO profiles without any modification of the gondola structure. Washers are used under all bolt heads to ensure proper load distribution.

A total of four independent mounting points are used between the base plate and the ESCARGO frame, providing redundancy and ensuring that the experiment remains securely attached in the event of a single fastener failure.

No modifications to the ESCARGO frame are required. A schematic diagram of the mechanical interface between the experiment, the aluminium base plate, and the ESCARGO frame is provided.

\section{Mechanical Design}


The mechanical design of the experiment consists of an external protective aluminium housing with dimensions of 100x100x50 mm and a wall thickness of 2 mm, as well as an internal pressurised chamber with dimensions of 38x33x45 mm and a wall thickness of 15 mm containing the K96 sensor, as shown in \fref{fig:CAD}. The total mass of the mechanical structure, excluding internal components, is approximately 800 g.

The internal pressurised chamber operates at pressures of up to 5 bar, while the external ambient pressure at float altitude can be as low as approximately 10 mbar, resulting in a maximum pressure differential of approximately 5 bar which is accounted for in the structural design.

The outer housing has one detachable wall fastened with four M2x10 screws, allowing access during integration and testing. The housing includes openings for air inlet and outlet for the pressurisation system, as well as internal mounting provisions for electrical components.

The internal pressurised chamber containing the K96 sensor includes dedicated interfaces for air inlet, air outlet aligned with the openings in the external housing, and electrical feedthroughs. One wall of the chamber is detachable and fastened with four M2x10 screws to allow sensor installation and servicing.

The pressurisation system is based on a pump used to increase the internal chamber pressure and an electronically controlled valve used to enable controlled flushing of the chamber.

Power distribution is handled by multiple LM2596HVS-ADJ step-down DC/DC regulators. The SAFT battery supplies a nominal voltage of 28.8 V, which is regulated down to the required system voltages of 3.3 V and 8 V.

\subsection{K96 Sensor}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{Graphics/Figures/K96 Sensor.png}
    \caption{K96 Sensor. Taken from \cite{wastine2022}}
    \label{fig:component_K96_sensor}
\end{figure}

The K96 sensor is a \ac{ndir} gas sensor capable of measuring methane, carbon dioxide, and water vapor. It operates from a 6.0--8.5 V VBB supply and a 2.4--3.6 V IOVCC/RxD logic supply over a temperature range of $-40\degree$C to +85\degree C, with a maximum peak current of 300 mA. The sensor also provides local temperature, pressure, and humidity measurements via an integrated BME280 sensor.

\subsection{Pump}
The pressurization system will include an electrically driven pump used to increase the pressure inside the measurement chamber. The pump will be powered from the experiment power distribution system and controlled by the processing unit.

At the current design stage, the specific pump model has not yet been selected. Candidate solutions include compact diaphragm- or piston-based pumps capable of operating at low inlet pressures and generating the required pressure differential for chamber pressurization. Final selection will be based on electrical power consumption, achievable pressure ratio, mass, and compatibility with low-temperature operation.

The pump will be operated in combination with an electronically controlled outlet valve to enable controlled air exchange of the measurement chamber. Detailed electrical characteristics and control parameters will be defined during the detailed design phase following component selection and testing.

\subsection{Ambient Pressure Sensor}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{Graphics/Figures/MS5607 Pressure Sensor.jpg}
    \caption{MS5607 Pressure Sensor}
    \label{fig:component_MS5607_pressure}
\end{figure}

The ambient pressure sensor MS5607 is a barometric sensor that provides a highly linear and precise 24-bit pressure and temperature value. \section{Thermal Design}

The temperature conditions in the stratosphere are quite extreme with temperatures reaching as low as -80\degree C at the altitudes of about 30 km we could reach. At the same time the air pressure can drop as low as 10 mbar, which also implies that convective heat transfer is very weak and most heat can be dissipated by radiative transfer only. This can lead to overheating of the electrical components if the heat from dissipated power accumulates. Therefore, it is essential to know how much power is dissipated and to take additional cooling measures in case that radiative cooling is insufficient.

The experiment can be thermally divided into three parts. The coldest part are the ambient sensors on the outside of the experiment box. They are and should be completely exposed and therefore are required to withstand temperatures as low as -80\degree C. This poses a challenge since most commercially available components are made for ground level conditions and manufacturers usually draw the line at -40\degree C for the temperature conditions. If we should get access to a thermal chamber, we can test the reliability of those sensors ourselves at lower temperatures. Since we haven't found sensors with operating ranges going down as low as -80\degree C we might have to resort to this attempt. Those sensors are not central to our experiment. The second part is the electronics compartment, which will likely be not actively heated. The bigger issue for this part might be overheating instead and a careful thermal analysis during the design process will be required to decide on mitigation steps if the temperature ranges of the components are exceeded at the upper or possibly still lower bounds. Later, in the integration process, further tests will follow to verify the functionality of that part at high altitudes.

The last part is the measurement chamber of which the temperature needs to be more carefully regulated to ensure that the K96 \ac{ndir} sensor, which is central to our experiment, functions properly. Temperature fluctuations can also lead to output drift and therefore we need to keep the temperature in a narrow range. At the same time, pumps, valves or inlet and outlet tube openings could freeze over if the humidity level is high. Since we might fly through clouds with very high humidity levels it is essential that the relative humidity doesn't get too high. By increasing the temperature of the air, condensation can be mitigated and freezing of pumps, valves and tubes should be prevented as well. Further tests will be conducted in later stages of experiment integration.

Insulation will play another essential role in the thermal design. The whole experiment should be as much thermally separated from the rest of the vehicle as possible. On one hand, this avoids further cooling of the experiment and on the other it avoids impacting other experiment or the vehicle. The measurement chamber should be well insulated as well to not (further) heat up the electronics compartment and to avoid condensation of water on the walls of the chamber.

\section{Software Design}

The on-board software is responsible for collecting data from the K96 sensor core and store it on the on-board SD card. It is also responsible for handling communication from and to ground, as well as the control of the internal pumping and thermal systems.

The settings of those systems depend on the readings of the internal and external pressure and temperature sensors. Additionally, correct flight phases need to be engaged. This is done based on longer term changes in the recorded ambient pressure or via a command from the ground, with the command taking priority. A information flow and process flow block diagram are provided in Figure \ref{fig:info_flow} and Figure \ref{fig:process_flow}.

To guarantee operations multiple \ac{mcu} will be included in the experiment with one being active while the other serving as a watchdog and can overtake operations if needed.

All transmissions are saved to the on-board storage with timestamps. Similarly, all saved data is saved with timestamps, too. A table detailing the expected data sizes of data to be stored is given in Table \ref{tab:data_rates}. This estimates around 3 kbps of stored data for a total duration of around 6 hours, giving a total storage requirement of 64.8 Mbit. Ideally the whole data is sent down in regular intervals once it is recorded. Based on the sampling rates in Table \ref{tab:data_rates}, an interval of every 30 seconds seems reasonable. Thus, every 30 seconds, 91.5 kbit need to be transmit, ideally within 10 seconds to provide room for sending checksums and possibly duplicates. Therefore, we estimate a normal data rate of 9.15 kbps. Assuming a maximum connection dropout duration of 10 minutes, this would likewise imply a build-up of data of 1.8 Mbit. Transferring it over the next 5 minutes gives a data rate of 60 kbps. With compression it is probable that only half the rates stated need to be transmitted, but a higher data rate could be used to send duplicates or checksums for error mitigation.

The K96 sensor core requires a UART interface and relies on the Modbus protocol. The communication protocol will be UDP.

\chapter{Data Analysis And Results}

\section{Data Analysis Plan}

The focus of attention for \ac{mirage} is to demonstrate the applicability of the \ac{ndir} sensor for low-cost and low-complexity methane measurements in the stratosphere. Since the biggest trade-off is resolution in this endeavour, we will assess the uncertainty of our measurement with great care. A lot of this will be done before the flight by carefully calibrating the sensor in the temperature and pressure range we plan on setting in the measurement chamber for different methane concentrations and humidity levels. If possible, we will also train a machine learning algorithm in this process to reduce the impact of noise on our measurements.

This is an important prerequisite for the quality of the data we can collect during the flight.

To further demonstrate the reliability of our measurement data, the focus of post-flight data analysis is on comparison of the vertical methane profile from ascent and descent with other sources of methane data. For this we want to use:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Radiative transfer models (like RTTOV)
    \item Chemistry and Transport models (like MOCAGE)
    \item Satellite data
    \item In-situ measurement data from other groups
    \item In-situ measurement with LIDAR at ESRANGE
\end{enumerate}

\ac{rtm} like RTTOV can provide reference concentration profiles of atmospheric gases like methane and have been compared to \ac{tdlas} measurements before. \ac{ctm} like MOCAGE assimilate satellite data into their calculations and give an additional gauge of what we should expect for our data, although they have shown worse correspondence with in-situ measurements \cite{joly2020}. The use of satellite data in addition to their indirect use via \ac{ctm} needs to be assessed. This and the familiarisation with \ac{rtm} and \ac{ctm} will be part of a bachelor thesis project within \ac{mirage}, that is planned for several team members.

In-situ measurements were conducted with \ac{tdlas} from ESRANGE in 2016 and provide a direct comparison with more expensive but also more accurate laser-based methods \cite{Khair2017}. The comparison is of course limited because of the different times at which measurements were done. We still expect to get some interesting insights by including these sources into our data analysis. Another in-situ measurement campaign conducted over France with AirCore in 2018 compared their data to \ac{ftir} measurements \cite{RINGO}. This is an interesting study for us as well, especially if ESRANGE can support us with LIDAR methane measurements. Whether this is possible is not clear yet but will be asked for.

As a secondary goal we want to investigate, whether a correlation between different landscapes and methane levels in the stratosphere can be drawn. Depending on the quality of our data and number of data points we collected during the floating phase of the flight, we will study fluctuations and compare them with the GPS data collected by the balloon. Since freshwater bodies, wetlands and industrial areas have higher methane emissions than for example forests, we are interested whether methane fluctuations in the stratosphere can be related to those different landscapes. Machine learning might be employed to help recognising those fluctuations.

\section{Results}

It is expected that we will receive less data points with a quality lower than other in-situ missions. However, if the calibration was done with the intended thoroughness, we expect that the results should be comparable and correspond well to \ac{rtm}. We expect to get good insights into current methane levels in the stratosphere and hope to inspire the further development and application of \ac{ndir} sensors for atmospheric monitoring.

There is a small chance that fluctuations of methane in the stratosphere can be connected to the emissions at ground level even if very precise measurement techniques are given. Especially, if we fly over different large areas like wetlands or lakes and forests, we might be able to detect changes in concentrations. Even if this goal fails, we will have collected methane concentration data with higher accuracy at about 25-30 km height due to the longer measurement time.

\section{Lessons Learned}
As a fresh and still growing team there were some lessons we could take away so far. 
\begin{enumerate}
  \item We took too much time to recruit new team members and still miss key team members for example in software design.
  \item We found out that we made wrong assumptions regarding the accuracy of the K96 sensor. This should have been prevented by asking more questions earlier on and closer communication with our contacts from Senseair.
  \item We need to take holidays into consideration and make more generous time estimates to prevent finishing crucial project steps like the \ac{sed} under pressure, which can lead to worse quality.
  \item We have to keep track of progress and check in with each sub team or team members working on specific tasks to prevent delays.
\end{enumerate}
Of course there are many more lessons of more practical nature. Everyone knows a little more about pumps and their limitations now, all of us learned something about methane sensing and many more things like that. However, the biggest lesson is probably that the documentation for such a project takes a lot of time and thought but also makes you aware of many aspects that can be easily overlooked.
