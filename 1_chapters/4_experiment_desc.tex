The experiment is built around the K96 \gls{ndir} sensor inside pressure chamber with continuously exchanging air (see \fref{fig:experimental_setup}).
In order to pressurize the chamber, \gls{mirage} will have two vacuum pumps and one piston compressor.
The first vacuum pump is taking ambient air and pressurizes it to around 0.5 bar.
The second vacuum pump then pressurizes 0.5 bar to 1 bar and the compressor from 1 bar to 3 bar. 
To monitor the settings of the pumps, temperature sensors mounted directly on the pumps, as well as temperature and pressure sensors between compression stages will be used.
The outlet of the pressure chamber is controlled using a valve to open/close the airflow.
Besides the sensors for the pressurization system, there are sensors for the thermal system and ambient sensors.\newline
The active components of the thermal system (heaters and coolers) will be controlled via a slave thermal \gls{mcu}.
Likewise, the pumps will be controlled via a slave pressure \gls{mcu}.
The main \gls{mcu} will act as a master for the other \gls{mcu}s and run the main part of the software.
Power will be provided via the connection to \gls{bexus} battery pack via a power connector and distributed to the system via a \gls{pdb}.

\subsection{Mechanical Interface}\label{mechanical_interface}

The \gls{mirage} enclosure is mechanically attached to the ESCARGO gondola structure via four mounting points located on its bottom plate. These four connection points interface with two parallel 45 $\times$ 45 mm Bosch aluminium T-slot profiles using M6 T-bolts, M6 rubber spacers, and M6 flange nuts. The parallel profiles are then mounted to the ESCARGO gondola structure using standard Rexroth M6 brackets, M6 T-bolts, and M6 flange nuts. This configuration facilitates installation without necessitating structural modifications to the ESCARGO frame, ensuring full compatibility with the standard mounting interface.

The mounting procedure for the enclosure is designed for simplicity and versatility. Due to the rectangular geometry of the assembly and the strategic placement of the internal insulation layer, mounting apertures can be integrated into the housing with high flexibility, without compromising the structural integrity of the chassis or the safety of internal components.
This modularity allows the experiment to be mounted on any face in various configurations.
While the current mounting scheme is illustrated in the provided documentation, the design remains adaptable to accommodate potential adjustments required by other experiments or spatial constraints.
To ensure effective mechanical decoupling, an elastomeric damping material (rubber) will be utilized at the interface between the enclosure and the gondola structure.
This isolation layer is intended to mitigate vibrational transmission, specifically addressing the sensitivity requirements and interference concerns raised by adjacent experimental payloads. 
It is also significant to note that the nested rectangular architecture of the assembly permits the axial rotation of Layer 3 relative to the external enclosure.
This modularity ensures that the spatial orientation of the primary system interfaces, specifically the Ethernet, power, and pneumatic inlet/outlet ports—can be adjusted to any face of the structure as required.
This design feature provides substantial flexibility, allowing the system to be reconfigured for different mission requirements or spatial constraints without necessitating fundamental structural modifications.

The mounting configuration provides a rigid mechanical interface between the experiment enclosure and the ESCARGO frame, ensuring structural redundancy and compliance with the standard ESCARGO mechanical interface requirements.

\subsection{Electrical Interface}\label{electrical_interface}

The electrical interface to the BEXUS power unit consists of the MIL-C 26482P series 1 connector, as provided by the user manual. Using pin A and B, the supply voltage from the SAFT MP176065 battery pack will be fed to the Power Distribution Board (PDB). The connector to the E-Link of the gondola will be an Amphenol RJF21B and the RJ45 connector will be employed for communication with an RJ45 plug on the \gls{mcu} \gls{pcb}. 

Since the BEXUS power supply is 28.8V, three step-down TSR3-2433N DC/DC converters and two linear regulators, LM317 and LM350, will be used on the PDB. For the K96 sensor 8V will be used, the pressurization system operates at 24V, the thermal system at 12V and 3.3V, and 3.3V for the sensors and \glspl{mcu}. The estimated average current draw from the BEXUS battery is 0.46A and the average power consumption is 13.13W, assuming a 7.5h flight. The total power consumption from the BEXUS power unit is 98.45Wh as listed in \sref{sec:power_design} and the peak current draw is 2.30A, assuming the thermal system is active. For protecting the BEXUS batteries and other experiments a pre-charge circuit will be implemented in order to limit the currents of the pressurization system and an LC filter to deal with the EMI and switching frequencies of DC/DC converters. An overcurrent safety measure will be implemented and will be controlled by the main \gls{mcu} and implemented using a current sensor and solid state relays. All subsystems will ground to the PDB and the PDB will ground to the minus-pin of the BEXUS battery line.

\subsection{Thermal}\label{thermal}
As outlined in \sref{sec: thermal design}, the thermal behaviour of the experiment will be controlled via heating elements and insulation. To avoid thermal coupling to ESCARGO, rubber bumpers between the experiment and the gondola will be used.
The thermal coupling will be further tested when the experiment is assembled. The air inlet and outlet for the air samples to be measured will be directed away from the vehicle and should not heat or cool the vehicle.

\subsection{Component-Level Electronics Description}

\subsubsection{K96 Sensor}

The K96 sensor is a \gls{ndir} gas sensor capable of measuring methane, carbon dioxide, and water vapor. It operates from a 6.0--8.5 V VBB supply and a 2.4--3.6 V IOVCC/RxD logic supply over a temperature range of $-40\degree$C to $+85\degree$C, with a maximum peak current of 300 mA. The sensor also provides local temperature, pressure, and humidity measurements via an integrated BME280 sensor.

\subsubsection{Temperature and Humidity Sensor}

The temperature and humidity sensor used for environmental monitoring within the system is the SHT45-AD1B-R2. The sensor operates over a temperature range of $-40$ to $125\degree$C and measures relative humidity in the range of 0--100\% RH. It provides a humidity accuracy of $\pm 1.0$\% RH and a temperature accuracy of $\pm 0.1\degree$C. Communication is performed via a digital I$^2$C interface, enabling direct integration with the processing unit. The sensor operates within a supply voltage range of 1.08 V to 3.6 V and has a typical average current consumption of approximately 0.4 $\mu$A.

The selection of the SHT45 was based on prior recommendations from the ESA committee. In addition, the sensor meets system requirements for accuracy, operating range, low power consumption, and digital communication, making it suitable for monitoring environmental conditions within the experiment.

\subsubsection{Ambient Temperature Sensor}

Ambient temperature monitoring is performed using the same SHT45 sensor chain, which provides temperature readout over the required environmental range.

\subsubsection{Temperature Sensor (Component Monitoring)}

The component temperature sensor used in the system is the TMP1075DGKR. The sensor measures temperature over a range of $-55$ to $125\degree$C and provides a digital output via an I$^2$C interface, enabling direct integration with the processing unit. It offers a typical accuracy of $\pm 0.5\degree$C (over a limited temperature range) and is designed for low-power operation. The sensor is implemented in a surface-mounted package, allowing close thermal contact with components on the PCB.

The TMP1075 is used to measure the surface temperature of selected components inside the experiment enclosure, specifically in contact with the SD-card reader on the main MCU board. The sensor was selected based on its low cost, I$^2$C communication protocol, and surface-mounted form factor, which simplifies integration and enables effective thermal coupling to the measured component. Additionally, the sensor supports configurable I$^2$C addressing, allowing up to 32 devices to be connected on the same bus if required.

This sensor enables monitoring of component temperatures during operation, supporting thermal analysis and verification that components operate within their allowable temperature limits.

\subsubsection{Pressure Sensors}

The pressure sensors used in the design are two sensors: MS580314BA and ABP2LANT030PG2A3XX.

The MS5803-14BA is an absolute pressure sensor providing a highly linear 24-bit digital output for both pressure and temperature. It supports communication via I$^2$C and SPI. The sensor was selected based on system requirements including the ability to measure pressures of up to 5 bar, use of I$^2$C communication, surface-mount compatibility, sufficient accuracy, and integrated temperature measurement. With a measurement range up to 14 bar, the sensor satisfies and exceeds the required operating range. It also meets the requirement of being available through Farnell, which is considered beneficial for procurement.

The ABP2LANT030PG2A3XX is a gauge pressure sensor providing a digital output via I$^2$C. This sensor is used specifically to measure the pressure between the vacuum pump and compressor. The sensor was selected based on its mechanical design, allowing mounting to a T-junction using off-the-shelf components, thereby reducing integration effort. It measures differential pressure relative to ambient in the range 0--30 psi (approximately 0--2 bar), which covers the expected operating conditions of approximately atmospheric pressure. The sensor has a lower accuracy, with a worst-case uncertainty of approximately 0.1 bar and a typical uncertainty of approximately 0.02 bar. This is considered sufficient, as the sensor is primarily used for system control and safety functions, such as regulating the compressor and preventing overpressure. Since the sensor measures gauge pressure, compensation using ambient pressure measurements is required.

\subsubsection{Current Sensor}

The ACS712 is a current sensor that consists of a linear Hall circuit integrated onto one of the PDB PCBs. The sensor is supplied using the 5 V line and has a current draw of 10 mA. The ambient operating temperature range is $-40$ to $85\degree$C, and the current rating is 5 A.

\subsubsection{DC/DC Converter}

The DC/DC converters used are the TRACO TSR3-24xxN for the 12 V, 5 V and 3.3 V lines. The TSR3-24 DC/DC converters have a switching frequency of 1000 kHz. Two linear regulators, LM317 and LM350, are used for the 8 V and 24 V lines, respectively. The former has an output voltage range of 1.25--33 V and a maximum current output of 1.5 A. The latter has an output voltage range of 1.2--33 V and a maximum current output of 3 A, which is required by the compressor and vacuum pumps.

\subsubsection{Main Microcontroller}

The microcontroller unit used in the system is the ESP32-WROOM. The MCU is based on a 32-bit architecture and operates at clock frequencies of up to 240 MHz. It provides integrated Wi-Fi and Bluetooth capabilities, as well as multiple communication interfaces including I$^2$C, SPI, and UART, enabling integration with the system sensors and subsystems. The module operates at a supply voltage of 3.3 V.

The MCU is used as the main processing unit of the system, responsible for sensor data acquisition, control logic, and communication between subsystems.

The selection of the ESP32 was based on its higher computational performance compared to the previously used Arduino Mega, as well as its wide range of communication interfaces and compatibility with the system requirements. Additionally, the module form factor simplifies integration and reduces development effort.

\subsubsection{Subsystem Microcontrollers (Thermal and Pressure Control)}

The thermal and pressure subsystems are each controlled by dedicated microcontrollers, implemented using the Arduino Nano ESP32. The board is based on a 32-bit ESP32-S3 microcontroller and operates at 3.3 V, providing sufficient processing capability for real-time control and data acquisition tasks. It supports multiple communication interfaces, including I$^2$C, SPI, and UART.

These microcontrollers are used for actuator control. By distributing these functions to dedicated units, the system reduces the computational load on the main MCU.

The selection of the Arduino Nano ESP32 was based on its compact form factor, availability, and compatibility with existing development tools. Additionally, it provides sufficient I/O capability and processing performance for subsystem-level control while simplifying implementation through its integrated design.

\subsubsection{MicroSD Card}

Measurement data is stored on a microSDHC memory card (SP016GBSTHBU1V10SP) with a capacity of 16 GB. The card has maximum read and write speeds of up to 85 MB/s and 15 MB/s, respectively. The device operates within a supply voltage range of 2.7--3.6 V and an operating temperature range of $0\degree$C to $70\degree$C.

The card interfaces with the processing core unit via SPI (Serial Peripheral Interface), using the MOSI, MISO, SCK, and CS signals for data transfer and control.

\subsubsection{Pressure Control Actuators}

The pressure control subsystem consists of a vacuum pump and a compressor used together to regulate and increase pressure within the system, as described in \sref{sec: pump system arch}.

The vacuum pump is a compact diaphragm pump operating on a 24 V supply and controlled via PWM, enabling adjustable operation.

The compressor is a DC motor operating on 24 V. It does not include an integrated control circuit and is therefore driven using a low-side MOSFET control circuit implemented on the pressure control PCB.

The selection of the vacuum pump and compressor was based on compatibility with system voltage, availability, and their combined ability to achieve the required pressure conditions. By operating these components together, the system enables controlled pressurization.

\subsubsection{Thermal Control Actuators}

The thermal control subsystem consists of a Peltier element and resistive heater films used to regulate temperature within the experiment enclosure.

The Peltier element is a thermoelectric module with dimensions of $40 \times 40$ mm, operated at 3.3 V to reduce current. It is used to provide active thermal control within the system by transferring heat when an electrical current is applied.

The heater elements are resistive heater films with dimensions of approximately $12 \times 39$ mm, operated at 12 V and 4.5 W. These elements generate heat through resistive losses and are used to provide controlled heating within the system.

The selection of the Peltier element and heater films was based on availability, simple electrical integration, and their ability to provide sufficient thermal control within the expected operating conditions.

\subsubsection{I2C Multiplexer}

A TCA9548APWR is used to manage communication on the I$^2$C bus. The device provides up to eight independent I$^2$C channels, allowing multiple devices with identical or limited address configurations to be connected without address conflicts.

The multiplexer communicates with the main MCU via the I$^2$C interface and is required in the system due to the use of multiple sensors with fixed or overlapping I$^2$C addresses.

The selection of the TCA9548A was based on compatibility with the system, simplicity of integration, and its ability to expand the I$^2$C bus while maintaining reliable communication.

\subsection{Experiment Enclosure}
\label{sec:enclosure}
The experimental enclosure is a composite structure consisting of three distinct functional layers.
The exterior layer comprises a thin protective polymer shell, designed to provide mechanical reinforcement for the underlying insulation.
The secondary layer serves as the primary thermal barrier, with a nominal thickness of 12.5 mm.
The final, innermost layer consists of a \gls{cots} aluminium enclosure featuring a wall thickness of 2 mm.
The overall envelope of the assembly is constrained to maximum external dimensions of 200x200x150 mm.\par

The primary objective of the first layer is to safeguard the intermediate insulation from structural degradation.
Due to the inherent mechanical vulnerability of the insulating material, this external cladding is necessary to prevent failure during handling and operation.
However, it should be noted that the plastic shell is not the principal defence against high-energy impacts; instead, the responsibility for maintaining structural integrity and absorbing kinetic loads during impact resides with the third layer (aluminium housing).\par

The second layer of the wall will be insulating material which aims to ensure the right temperature inside the experiment in order to avoid any possible temperature deviations and component failure. 
The selection of the thermal insulation material is informed by material property charts that correlate mechanical strength with density, allowing for the identification of the most suitable candidate for the experimental requirements.
Based on these comparative diagrams, polymeric foams were identified as the optimal material class, as they provide an ideal balance between required structural integrity and superior thermal insulation properties.
The precise specification of the foam has not yet been finalized, as the dimensions of the insulation layer are functionally dependent on the final volume of the third layer (internal housing).
An expansion of the third layer’s dimensions would necessitate a proportional reduction in insulation thickness; conversely, a reduction in the internal assembly's footprint would permit an increased volume for the thermal barrier.
Despite these potential dimensional refinements, current material analysis confirms that polymeric foams will remain the baseline choice for the insulation architecture, see also \fref{fig:thermal conduct chart insulation}.\par

The third layer will be the commercial off the shelf aluminium enclosure with an expected wall thickness of 2 mm in which all the components will be placed.
We chose to use a \gls{cots} enclosure in order to reduce manufacturing complexity which also helps with our budget and makes the whole experiment safer.
Its main purpose is to house the electronics and provide a good protection.
The enclosure chosen is IP certified and was tested in extreme environments close to stratospheric ones.
It also comes with pre-made holes that allow us to connect experiment to the E-link system and \gls{bexus} power while ensuring a leak tight connection.\par 

The whole enclosure (counting in three layers), not counting the mandatory connections, will have one inlet holes for the vacuum pump to suck in the air and one exit hole for the air to escape. Airflow is fully isolated from the enclosure and routed exclusively through sealed tubing. This ensures that only the measurement chamber is pressurized, in compliance with requirement D.8.\par

\subsection{Internal Layout}
The internal layout of the \gls{mirage} experiment is arranged to maintain a compact configuration in order to minimize space taken in all the axis.
Currently, the system is configured such that two vacuum pumps are vertically stacked in the upper-left corner to optimize space utilization along the Z-axis, where spatial constraints are more significant than in the X and Y dimensions.
These pumps subsequently supply air to a compressor situated in the lower-left corner, which is positioned in the immediate vicinity of the pressure chamber to minimize the distance between the air discharge and the chamber inlet.
The pressure chamber is located as close as possible to the solenoid valve.
This proximity is maintained to minimize the total pressurized volume; notably, the tubing connecting the chamber to the valve features a reduced diameter of 2 mm, in contrast to the 6 mm diameter utilized in the remainder of the system.
Adjacent to the primary assembly is a PCB tower, which provides a compact arrangement for the circuit boards, isolating them from the pneumatic conduits and minimizing the footprint that would otherwise be required for individual, planar placement.

\subsection{Pressurized Measurement Chamber}

The pressurized chamber is designed to accommodate a K96 sensor alongside an auxiliary pressure sensor and potentially an auxiliary temperature sensor.
The internal layout is providing sufficient volume to house the printed circuit boards (PCBs) for both the pressure and K96 sensors.
Commercial off-the-shelf (COTS) components proved unsuitable for this application due to stringent requirements regarding a sanitized internal environment and the necessity for minimal internal volume to optimize airflow.
Consequently, the chamber will be custom-manufactured using university facilities.
Drawing upon established automotive engineering methodologies, a copper gasket was selected for the sealing interface.
Copper is a ductile metal capable of undergoing plastic deformation under sufficient mechanical load, thereby creating a hermetic and waterproof seal.
The internal geometry focuses on minimizing the displacement distance between the inlet and the outlet. 
%Although initial design iterations aimed to minimize total dead space, it was subsequently concluded that internal volume is of secondary importance, provided that the airflow remains rapid and free from turbulence. 
%This laminar flow is maintained by positioning the inlet and outlet on opposing walls in the immediate vicinity of the K96 sensor.
To reduce assembly complexity, electrical connections are routed through hermetically sealed apertures, as managing cables through such interfaces is more efficient than utilizing the standard connectors provided by the sensors.
The interface will be a bulkhead-feedthrough connector like the MIL-C 26482P series 1. 
The wall thickness was calculated to be 5 mm on sides/top and 3 mm on bottom, ensuring safe operation at a differential pressure of 3 bar without the risk of structural failure or deformation.\par

\gls{fea} was conducted to validate the structural integrity of the chamber under internal pressure loading, see \fref{fig:fea_static_chamber}.
The results show a maximum stress of approximately $1.57 \cdot 10^7$ Pa, a maximum displacement of $2.4\cdot10^{-3}$ mm, and a factor of safety of approximately 39.
These results confirm that the chamber operates well within material limits.
The chamber is assembled using 6 x M3 fasteners with preload, ensuring that the sealing force exceeds the internal pressure force acting on the lid.
Given that copper gaskets undergo permanent deformation and are therefore single-use, elastomeric seals will be utilized during the preliminary testing phase. 
However, for the final flight hardware intended for stratospheric conditions, copper is mandatory due to its superior thermal resilience. 
In scenarios where pressure drops, or the experiment fails, internal temperatures are expected to reach $-60$ °C.
At such extremes, rubber seals reach their glass transition temperature, becoming brittle and losing their sealing properties.
Conversely, copper maintains its structural integrity and, under adequate clamping force, provides a superior seal compared to elastomers.
The pressure chamber is CNC-machined from an aluminium block, while the gasket is fabricated from a thin copper sheet tailored to the chamber's perimeter.
The sealing mechanism employs a ridge-and-groove interface between the lid and the body, where a semicircular ridge compresses the copper into a matching groove to ensure a robust seal.

\subsection{Pump System Architecture}\label{sec: pump system arch}

The \gls{mirage} experiment utilizes a multi-stage air circulation system to maintain a controlled internal pressure while ensuring a continuous exchange of air within the measurement chamber.

\textbf{System Operation:}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Pre-heating: Ambient intake air is pre-heated before entering the vacuum stage to prevent condensation or freezing within the system components.
    \item Vacuum Stage: Two diaphragm vacuum pumps operate continuously from launch up to an altitude of 22 km.
    These pumps supply the compressor with air at approximately atmospheric pressure ($\approx$ 1 bar).
    Since the volumetric flow rate increases with decreasing intake pressure, the pumps will operate at higher settings at higher altitudes. The vacuum pumps will operate in an inline configuration where the first vacuum pump provides pre-pressurized air for the second.
    \item Intercooling: To manage thermal loads generated by compression, the tubing connecting the vacuum pumps to the piston compressor serves as a passive intercooler.
    \item Compression Stage: A piston compressor increases the airflow pressure to 3 bar before it enters the measurement chamber.
    \item Pressure Regulation: The internal chamber pressure is maintained by an outflow valve equipped with a shutter mechanism. This valve regulates the exhaust flow rate to the external environment. The valve is per default closed, opening only after electrical power is applied. A potential model is 2V025-1/4.
    \item Monitoring: Integrated pressure and temperature sensors are situated within the tubing between compression stages to provide real-time performance data and ensure stable operation.
\end{enumerate}

The nominal operating pressure inside the chamber is expected to reach approximately 3 bar above perfect vacuum. At float altitude, the external ambient pressure may decrease to approximately 10 mbar, resulting in a pressure differential of approximately 3 bar across the chamber walls. This pressure differential is accounted for in the structural design of the chamber.
During standard operation, the pressure between the vacuum pumps and the compressor stage is maintained near atmospheric levels at 1 bar to optimize the volumetric efficiency of the compressor.

Environmental conditions at float altitude necessitate the implementation of an intake air pre-heating system prior to the vacuum stage; this measure it is essential to mitigate the risk of condensation or cryogenic desublimation (icing) within the pneumatic circuits.
Additionally, pressure transducers are integrated between the compression stages to monitor real-time system performance and ensure precise pressure regulation.
The system architecture employs a series (staged) configuration of the vacuum pumps to maximize the total volumetric flow rate l/min.
In this staged configuration, the primary pump compresses the intake air to an intermediate pressure of 0.5 bar, which the secondary pump subsequently elevates to 1 bar.
This series-staged approach is estimated to be more efficient for the specific hardware utilized in this experiment, providing a performance increment of approximately 300 mL/min over a parallel configuration.
The outlet is implemented as a micro-orifice with a diameter of approximately 0.015 mm, designed to achieve choked flow conditions.
At a pressure ratio of approximately 0.53, the flow reaches Mach 1 at the outlet, ensuring that the mass flow rate becomes independent of downstream pressure.
The orifice will be implemented using a precision micro-orifice or laser-drilled insert.
Further information on flow rates can be found in \aref{sec:analysis flow rates} \fref{fig:flow_curves_dia_pump}.\par 

\gls{cfd} simulations were performed to evaluate flow behaviour and pressure distribution within the chamber.
Results show stable internal pressure ($\sim3$ bar), a controlled pressure drop across the outlet, and near-sonic flow conditions at the outlet.
Early designs exhibited turbulence near sharp edges and screw features, which were mitigated through geometry refinement.
Simulations were conducted using compressible flow and representative pressure boundary conditions.\par 

The \gls{cfd} simulations are complimenting the analytical estimations of the outlet hole size.
In particular, multiple steady state simulations were run to evaluate the impact that the inlet's dimension has on the system.
Larger inlet diameters resulted in lower fluid velocities and lower pressures. Smaller inlets had a reversed impact on these values. 
The average pressure inside the pressure chamber remained largely the same upon changing the inlet dimensions at 3 bars.
As the mass flow rate on both the inlet and outlet was influenced by the inlet's dimensions, the study focused on choosing a suitable inlet to stabilize the pressure inside the chamber.
To give an example, using an inlet dimension of 2 mm, the resulting system had a pressure leak of $0.0181\cdot 10^{-4} \,\mathrm{g/s}$.
The system was simulated with the atmospheric conditions at an altitude of 30 km with 0.011 bar and -80 °C. 
The volumetric flow rate of the inlet was simulated to be 0.2 litre per minute.
The outlets boundary condition was the atmospheric conditions.
The results of the \gls{cfd} simulation mentioned above is shown in \fref{fig:CFD_flow_rates}.
The analysis shows that the air exits the chamber close to Mach 1, as estimated analytically.
However, the outlet diameter is much larger than theoretically estimated. 
This indicates that further simulations and tests are needed to determine the final outlet size.

\subsection{Pump and Fluid Handling Technologies}

In contemporary industrial practice, the sampling and measurement of small-volume gas and liquid specimens typically rely on compact pumping systems.
These systems generally utilize two primary technologies: piston-driven and diaphragm-based architectures, each offering distinct advantages tailored to specific operational requirements.
Diaphragm pumps are the most widely implemented technology, utilized in a spectrum of applications ranging from standard pneumatic tools to high-precision medical instrumentation.
These pumps operate by oscillating a flexible diaphragm using an electric motor, creating alternating suction and compression cycles.
Because the gas is hermetically separated the mechanical drive components by the diaphragm, diaphragm pumps operate oil-free and are resistant to contamination.
This makes them particularly suitable for experiments involving sensitive sensors or atmospheric gas sampling.
They are characterized by high operational reliability and exceptional cost-effectiveness; their fundamental working principle is straightforward and does not necessitate the use of ultra-high-precision components.
The primary technical limitation of diaphragm pumps, however, is their relatively low compression ratio when compared to alternative mechanical designs.
In contrast, piston pumps are engineered to achieve significantly higher discharge pressures. 
Besides higher losses due to increased friction, the complexity of the piston assembly requires superior material grades and high-precision manufacturing compared to vacuum pumps, resulting in increased capital costs.
Consequently, piston pumps are typically reserved for high-pressure applications, as they offer few competitive advantages over diaphragm pumps in low-pressure scenarios.
Other pumping technologies, such as rotary vane and scroll pumps, are frequently integrated into laboratory-grade vacuum systems where they provide superior ultimate pressures and higher volumetric flow rates.
These systems necessitate external lubrication, larger physical assemblies, and substantial power consumption.
These characteristics render them unsuitable for the current system integration, as the primary design objectives prioritize the minimization of total mass, power draw, and potential sample contamination.
Based on this comparative analysis, the system was designed using a hybrid configuration that combines both diaphragm and piston pump technologies.
This approach leverages the specifics of both architectures, ensuring high-purity, contaminant-free sampling while maintaining low power consumption and a lightweight assembly.\par 

Following the analysis above, the vacuum pumps are chosen to be the G4-4 Series Diaphragm Pumps from Pengpu.
The compressor is chosen to be the W6 Series piston pump from Pengpu.

\subsection{Tubing and Flow Control Components}

Gas flow regulation in compact atmospheric sensing instruments is traditionally achieved through the use of miniature solenoid or needle valves. Solenoid valves are often preferred for their rapid response times and ability to provide high-precision, electronically controlled flow regulation within automated systems.\\
Therefore an electrical valve is chosen, which is located near the exhaust port. This valve is configured as 'normally closed.' In the event of experiment shutdown or transition into standby mode — where power is prioritized for the electronics and SD card heating system — the valve remains sealed. In the case of a sudden pressure drop due to a pump system failure or temporary power outage this could mean that a high pressure is retained and operation can potentially continue. Furthermore, this serves as a fail-safe against a potential landing in water to protect the electronics.\\
The electrical valve we chose is first of all a very tough component, capable of withstanding thousand kilopascals of pressure or 10\,bar. It can also withstand a temperature of -10°C to positive 60°C. But it works on a very simple basis, where a magnet is natively pulling the valve shut to stop the flow of air, gas or liquid. If it is powered a second stronger magnet turns on and pulls up the pin an opens the valve. This is a very simple non-invasive addition which doesn't complicate the electrical design much and consumes very little power for the safety it gives.

\subsection{Tubing Material and Fluid Connections}

In compact gas circulation systems, tubing connections are typically achieved using barbed fittings, compression fittings, or push-connect pneumatic fittings. Barbed fittings are commonly used with flexible tubing materials such as silicone or polyurethane, where the tubing is pressed onto the fitting and secured through friction or hose clamps. Compression fittings provide a more robust and leak-tight connection and are frequently used in higher-pressure gas systems. Push-to-connect fittings are widely used in pneumatic systems due to their ease of installation and reliable sealing.\\
For the MIRAGE experiment, the tubing connections will be selected to ensure leaktight operation at pressures up to approximately 3 bar while maintaining compatibility with the chosen tubing material and the compact geometry of the experimental setup. Particular attention will be given to minimizing leakage and dead volumes in the system to maintain stable pressure control and reliable airflow through the measurement chamber.\\
Regarding the concrete material, the system uses polyurethane (PU) tubing with quick-connect fittings. PU was selected due to its flexibility, allowing tight routing within the compact geometry, and its compatibility with pneumatic fittings. It meets the required pressure range (tested up to 6 bar) and operates within the temperature range of -25 to 85°C in the system. Therefore, the selection of specialized or exotic materials for the tubing is deemed unnecessary. One aspect that has to be kept in mind however is that the tubing material should not off gas for example volatile organic compounds (VOCs) that could disturb the atmospheric measurements. This aspect needs to be researched more before fully deciding on PU.

\subsection{Structural Considerations}

As detailed in the preceding \autoref{sec:enclosure}, the enclosure consists of three different layers that serve protection, insulation and mechanical robustness. The structurally most significant layer is the inner layer of the enclosure. It needs to provide mechanical integrity, shock and vibration resilience. To meet these criteria, a commercial off-the-shelf (COTS) component was selected, which has been rigorously tested and was certified according to the Ingress Protection (IP) rating system.\\

Since the experiment does not consist of internal layers, an analysis was conducted to determine the optimal materials for each stage. The forces considered during this evaluation were primarily those encountered during the nominal flight profile. Consequently, the design of the primary outer shell and the insulation layer do not account for landing impact loads, as these components are assumed to be sacrificial and unlikely to maintain structural integrity upon contact with the ground.\\
The outer layer, can be fabricated from cost-effective polymers according to current calculations. This material offers sufficient structural strength for stratospheric flight, and provided the correct polymer is selected, it will not undergo cryogenic embrittlement or structural failure at low temperatures. A final decision between a polymer-based or aluminum shell remains pending. The shell itself does not perform a critical safety function, as its sole purpose is to protect the underlying insulation.\\
The innermost layer, which houses all critical instrumentation, will be constructed from aluminum. Its primary objective is to safeguard the experiment against impact forces and potential water immersion. This component will be a commercially procured enclosure meeting or exceeding IP66 standards. This selection is final, as maintaining a waterproof seal and preventing structural breach during landing are critical requirements.
The combination of these material phases results in a robust experimental assembly. The insulating foam layer in the middle acts as an energy absorber, providing significant impact attenuation during landing, while the internal aluminum housing ensures a rigid, waterproof environment for the components. This multi-layered architecture allows the experiment to endure various landing scenarios with minimal risk of internal hardware damage. \\
Another component that has to endure high mechanical loads is the pressure chamber inside the enclosure. Its design requires a hermetic (air-tight) seal utilizing a copper gasket. The system will be operated under a pressure of 3\,bar but needs to be tested up to 4.5\,bar to comply with safety regulations at ESRANGE and from the BEXUS program. Preliminary Finite Element Analysis (FEA) conducted within the Solidworks environment indicates that a 5 mm wall thickness for the pressure chamber is entirely sufficient, providing a substantial factor of safety. This assessment assumes an internal operating pressure of 3 bar acting against an external vacuum. \section{Electronics Design}

The overall electronics architecture of the MIRAGE experiment is illustrated in Figure 4.8. The design is centered around a Power Distribution Board that interfaces with the BEXUS gondola power supply. This board monitors current consumption and distributes regulated voltage levels to all subsystems within the experiment.

At the core of the system is an ESP32-based main control unit, responsible for coordinating sensor data acquisition, executing control logic, and managing communication with onboard storage and peripheral modules. The control unit interfaces with multiple subsystems, including the pressurization system, thermal control system, the K96 NDIR sensor core, and a set of environmental sensors.

The environmental sensors measuring ambient temperature, pressure, and humidity are connected to the main control unit via an I$^2$C bus through a multiplexer, allowing multiple sensors with identical addresses or a large number of devices to be handled efficiently. In contrast, the K96 NDIR sensor communicates with the control unit through a UART interface. Additional interfaces and signal routing, such as PWM-controlled actuators and analog sensing lines, are also integrated as shown in the schematic.

\subsection{Power Distribution Board}

Power distribution within the experiment is handled by a dedicated Power Distribution Board (PDB). The board receives a nominal input voltage of 28.8 V from the BEXUS power system and distributes regulated voltages to the experiment subsystems. The voltage regulation will be implemented using three TSR3-2433N DC/DC converters and two linear regulators, LM317 and LM350. The TRACO TSR3 has a high efficiency (~90+\%) and requires fewer complementary components but cannot provide more than 15 V and will therefore be used for lower voltage levels such as 3.3 V. The LM317 will be used for the 8 V line and has an adjustable 1.25--33 V output and an output current of 1.5 A. The 24 V line is required to handle higher currents due to the vacuum pumps and compressor and will therefore use the LM350 with an adjustable output voltage range of 1.2--33 V and an output current of 3 A.

The five required voltage levels of the experiment are 24 V for the two vacuum pumps and compressor, 12 V for the various heaters, 8 V for the K96 sensor, 5 V for the current sensor, and 3.3 V for the sensors, Peltier and microcontrollers.

To mitigate the switching noise introduced by the DC/DC converters on the shared power lines, an LC filter with a cut-off frequency of 5 kHz will be implemented on the power distribution board, see \fref{fig:pdb_schematic}. The TRACO TSR3 DC/DC converters also have a third-order CLC filter in accordance with the recommended application notes of the TSR3. The cut-off frequency for this filter is 33.9 kHz, also shown in \fref{fig:pdb_schematic}.

The vacuum pumps and compressor have high inrush currents when powered on, and to mitigate this a pre-charge circuit is integrated on the PDB PCB on the 24 V line. The pre-charge circuit consists of two back-to-back SIS176LDN NMOS. The main MCU provides the required gate voltage of 3.3 V for the NMOS in series with the 30 ohm resistor to limit the inrush current and then switches to the Q3 and Q6 NMOS in \fref{fig:pdb_schematic}.

Due to the potential high current draw from the BEXUS batteries, a current measurement sensor ACS712 is located after the backup LC filter. The mechanism for limiting current if it becomes too high is done by sending an analog signal to the main MCU, which in turn can disable each power rail and thus limit the current draw. The caveat is that the 3.3 V rail cannot be limited in this way due to it supplying the main MCU. The disabling of power lines is done with an S102S01 solid state relay.

\subsection{Microcontroller Unit}

The main control unit of the MIRAGE system is centered around the ESP32-C5-WROOM-1, which serves as the primary processing unit responsible for reading, collection, management and storage of data from the K96 as well as both ambient and internal temperature, pressure and humidity sensors. The data from the sensors will be stored on the SD-card. It will also handle communication with the ground station using the provided E-link connection onboard the BEXUS gondola.

As shown in \fref{fig:mcu_pcb}, the ESP32-C5 is powered through the 3.3 V rail from the power distribution board. Decoupling capacitors are placed on the power rail to ensure stable operation.

Communication with peripherals is handled through multiple interfaces. The SPI interface is used for communication with the SD card and the Ethernet module, enabling both data storage and communication with the ground station. The I$^2$C bus is used to interface with sensors and subsystem microcontrollers. To allow multiple devices with identical I$^2$C addresses, a TCA9548A I$^2$C multiplexer is implemented, which divides the bus into multiple selectable channels.

A DS3231 real-time clock is connected to the I$^2$C bus to provide accurate timekeeping for timestamping sensor data. The MCU PCB also includes multiple connectors and test points, providing access to power and communication lines for integration with external sensors and subsystem control units.

The main MCU also has the capabilities to disable power to all but the 3.3 V line in case of overcurrent.

In addition to its primary functions, the MCU also implements system monitoring and fault recovery mechanisms. It acts as a software watchdog for the pressure and thermal MCUs, ensuring that these subsystems operate correctly and respond as expected. In the event of irregular behavior or loss of communication, the MCU can initiate appropriate recovery actions. To further enhance system reliability, a dedicated hardware watchdog is included for the main MCU, providing automatic reset functionality in case of software faults or system hangs.

\subsection{Thermal System}

The thermal system is designed to control the temperature of critical components within the MIRAGE experiment and consists of both passive and active elements. The active thermal system is implemented on a dedicated thermal control PCB centered around a thermal MCU, implemented using an ESP32-S3 Nano, which operates as a slave to the main MCU. The thermal MCU receives sensor data and control signals from the main MCU through the I$^2$C interface and uses this information to regulate thermal conditions throughout the system. It contains the control logic for the active thermal system and supports multiple operating modes, including PID control, simple control, manual control, and an off mode, providing flexibility and redundancy in case of unexpected behavior or system failure. The PID parameters will be determined through experimental testing at a later stage.

Temperature sensors are placed at key locations within the experiment, including the air inlet, the pipe between the vacuum pump and compressor, the pressure chamber, and the SD-card. These sensors are read by the main MCU, which forwards the relevant data to the thermal MCU. As the sensors are distributed throughout the system, several dedicated PCB designs are used to accommodate different sensor types and mounting locations.

Control of heating and cooling elements is achieved through PWM signals generated by the thermal MCU. These signals are applied to the gates of IRLZ44N logic-level N-channel MOSFETs, which act as low-side switches for the active components. Power is supplied directly from the power distribution board to the heating and cooling elements, while the MOSFETs control the current path to ground. Gate resistors and pull-down resistors are included to ensure stable switching behavior, and the MOSFETs are arranged on the PCB to allow for heat sinking if required.

\subsubsection{Cold case}\label{sec: thermal cold case}
The cold case is a steady state assumption, given environmental conditions at a specific altitude. The appropriate altitude is determined as the maximum of the air heat convection $Q_\mathrm{conv}(h)$ profile from the averaged temperature and pressure profiles given in \fref{fig:temp_press_profile}. It is assumed that no solar radiation is impacting the system (except \gls{ir} radiation from the Earth). Ambient pressure is taken from \fref{fig:temp_press_profile}, while the ambient temperature is set to be $-80$°C. Notably, the tubes containing pressurized, hot air, are assumed to be perfect thermal insulators, which is far from accurate. However, as the heat flux provided by the heated air is very small, this is acceptable.\par 
To estimate impact of different operations and system designs, the analysis takes into account whether pumps are turned on or off, whether the experiment has heaters or not and whether the experiment is insulated or not. The insulation is assumed to be a 5 cm \dos{adjust} thick box of styrofoam, completely encapsulating the chassis. The pumps are assumed to draw 50 W. This corresponds to the maximum power draw possible by two diaphragm pumps and one piston compressor. The active pumps were chosen according to the operations schedule as outlined in \sref{sec: pump system arch}. Assuming an efficiency of 60\% for the diaphragm pumps and 40\% for the piston compressor, the resulting heat load is 26 W. \dos{adjust}\par

\subsubsection{Hot case}\label{sec: thermal hot case}
Similar to the cold case, the hot case will follow a lumped system approach. However, the appropriate altitude here is chosen to minimize the impact of heat convection via air and maximizing solar heating. This means that the hot case will assume environmental conditions like those found at 30 km altitude, namely $T=-66$°C, $p=0.01$ bar and cloud-free skies. As in the cold case analysis, the effects of pumps, active heating, and styrofoam insulation are tested for individually. The results are displayed in \tref{tab:thermal results hot case}. Without styrofoam insulation, and no thermal system/heating or active pumps, the chassis temperature will reach around $-3$°C. 
Adding the insulation leads to a counterintuitive temperature reduction to $-47$°C. This is due to the styrofoam reflecting more sunlight than the aluminium, while simultaneously loosing heat more efficiently via \gls{ir} radiation. 
As the heat loads involved are comparatively small, an active heating system has to provide around 14 W to raise the chamber temperature to 21 °C. However, if pumps are active at 30 km altitude, the chassis will quickly reach temperatures higher than 21 °C, requiring cooling of around 9 W instead.\par 

In conclusion, the hot case analysis shows that active pumps at high altitudes will necessitate a cooling system. In order to avoid complexity, it is therefore favourable to avoid using the pumps at high altitudes. 
This agrees well with the planned operations for the pumps, as given in \sref{sec: pump system arch}.

\subsubsection{Continuous operation}
As before, the continuous operation uses a lumped system approach. However, now the total energy of the system is not assumed to be constant, but transient. This means that the system temperature is changing with the changing environmental and internal heat loads. Therefore, thermal inertia needs to be taken into account via
\begin{align}
    mc_{p}\dot{T} =& Q_\mathrm{ele}+Q_\mathrm{pump}(h)+Q_\mathrm{transfer} + Q_\mathrm{rad\,in}(h) + Q_\mathrm{insulation} - Q_\mathrm{conv}(h) - Q_\mathrm{rad\,out} \label{eq: heat diff chassis}
\end{align}
for the chassis. As before, the power required to keep the pressure chamber at 21 °C can be calculated via \eqref{eq:heat_balance_chamber}, since $\dot{T}$ is supposed to be equal to 0 for the chamber. 
In this scenario the pumps operate by the operating plan specified in \sref{sec: pump system arch}. As described in \sref{sec:campaign_preparations}, the pressurization system will be used to pressurize the chamber to 3 bar around 3 hours before launch. The pumps will therefore only start at launch. Between 0 km and 22 km, the pumps operate at full power, adding a heat load of 26 W. Beyond 22 km the pumps are switched off. The total flight time is estimated to be 6 h long.
The float phase is assumed to be at constant 26 km altitude. The heating system is assumed to be capable of a maximum heating output of 10 W.
After the pumps are deactivated at 22 km, the heating system aims to stabilize the chamber temperature at $-5$°C. This is done as a proxy of the heaters active in the PCB stack \dos{reference component ID} to prevent the electronics in the chassis from freezing.\par 

The result for an experiment inside a styrofoam insulation box and subject to solar daylight is shown in \fref{fig:continous thermal sim main}.

\subsubsection{Conclusion of thermal analysis}\label{sec: thermal conclusion}
The cold and hot case analysis shows that the experiment if enclosed in a styrofoam insulation is able to stay within the required temperature range depending on the proper use of pumps. In the ascend phase, the pumps are generating enough heat to keep the chamber temperature at over 21 °C, even without additional heating elements. In fact the transient analysis shows that temperatures of chassis and chamber might reach between 30 °C to 40 °C. During float phase, the chamber temperature is allowed to drop to $-5$°C. This is done in order to keep the electrical components within the chassis at around $-10$°C. The required total heating power for daytime and nighttime would be $\approx 26.79$ Wh and $\approx 40.11$ Wh, respectively. Depending on the final design of the electrical components, the temperature limit and thus required power might be lowered further.
The real insulation material will likely not be styrofoam but another foam \dos{reference material selection analysis}.

\subsubsection{Passive thermal control system}
There are three elements of the passive control system. The specifics of each need to be determined through further testing.

\paragraph{The insulation} material is still to be decided, as is its exact dimensions.
This simulation estimates a thickness of 21 mm on the sides and 13 mm on the top and bottom. The real insulation is expected to be of a similar size.

\paragraph{The intake air preheater} is necessary for the vacuum pump to receive air at temperatures greater than 0 °C and can be either active or passive.
The design as shown in \dos{ref overview schematic} includes the active heater and is the currently chosen design approach.
However, further testing might show that a passive system is sufficient. 
In this case, the tubing delivering ambient air to the first vacuum pump would have a high enough heat capacity to act as a heat sink for the incoming ambient air. 
The required length, shape, and material of the tubing would need to be determined via tests.
Additionally, a spring turbulator might be placed inside the tube to increase the heat transfer efficiency.

\paragraph{The thermal bridges for the pumps} might be necessary to cool the pumps down.
Under prolonged use the pumps reach around 50 °C at room temperature and surface level pressure.
When pressure decreases at higher altitude convective cooling decreases and the pumps are therefore expected to become even hotter.
To prevent overheating, the pumps might need to be cooled using thermal bridges consisting of \dos{aluminium} plates connected to the enclosure and to the pumps using thermal pads. 

\subsubsection{Active thermal control system}
The above analysis shows that during ascend and before launch, the pumps alone will provide enough heating power to heat the chamber to around 40 °C. This is on the edge of the acceptable temperature range as outlined in \tref{tab:performance_req}, P.7. Therefore, coolers as well as heaters are needed. The exact specifications of which experiment parts need to be heated and which need to be cooled will be determined through future tests. The system design is including both heating and cooling functionalities where necessary.\par

During float and descend, the above analysis shows that a 10 W heater is sufficient to keep the electronics inside the chassis within acceptable limits, see \tref{tab:design_req}, D.1.3. There are multiple common choices for thermal control components at low wattages and high precision. These will be discussed below.

\emph{\gls{ptc} ceramics} are commonly used in portable space heaters. They are used via resistive heating. However, due to their \gls{ptc}, a rising internal temperature causes their resistivity to increase as well. This behaviour can be configured, such that a desired maximum temperature can be set at which the ceramic resistivity becomes too high for the temperature to rise further. They are therefore self-regulating, adding additional security and requiring less controlling. They are also quite cheap. However, because they are made of ceramic, they can not easily fit inside a tight experiment. 

\emph{Peltier devices} are heat pumps that transfer heat from one side of the device to the other when an electrical current is applied. Upon reversal of the current polarity, the heat transfer flow is also reversed. These devices can therefore be used to cool and heat. This makes them great candidates for an active thermal control system that has to cool and heat components depending on mission phase. The downside is that they require heat sinks that the excess heat (or lack thereof) can be dumped to. They also require more sophisticated controlling software and circuits to be able to switch between cooling and heating.

\emph{Polyimide film heating elements} consist of a thin conductive heating element surrounded by polyimide film. They are very flexible, sturdy and commonly used in space environment heating solutions. Although they are not self-regulating, they can be easily controlled using \gls{pid} algorithms. They are unable to provide cooling, but offer a great surface area to enable uniform heating. Due to their flexibility, they are also easy to integrate into a system and do not require complex software or circuits.\par  


Currently, Peltier devices as well as polyimide film strips are both included in the design to provide fine temperature control at different points in the experiment.
While \gls{ptc} ceramics are not included, fixed-value ceramic resistive heaters are.
This is done to make the control easier.
\tref{tab: comp active thermal control} gives an overview of the different heaters and coolers currently planned.
With further tests, some of these components may be removed, however, no new ones shall be added.
In the following a brief description and justification of every component will be given.

\section{Power design}
\label{sec:power_design}

The \gls{mirage} team expects to rely exclusively on the 28.8V SAFT MP176065 which is provided by the gondola.

The experiment will have an estimated average current of 0.46A and consume a total of 98.45Wh, see \tref{tab:power_full_flight_duty}, including the thermal system and the pressurization system. Previous estimates have not taken into account duty cycles of components but these numbers do. The experiment peak current, assuming duty cycles and thermal and pressurization systems are active at the same time, is 2.30A and occurs during the ascent phase of the flight when measurements are being taken, see \tref{tab:power_ascent_peak}. The rationale is to make sure that the air entering the pumps is at a functional temperature, that the gas in the measurement chamber is within the temperature range of requirement X.X and that the outlet does not freeze. Due to these facts, the worst-case scenario is that the thermal system has to be activated at the same time as the pumps and compressor. \tref{tab:power_ascent_no_thermal} shows the optimistic case that no heaters or coolers are needed during the ascent phase and the peak current is then 1.87A and a total consumption of 67.38Wh from the BEXUS batteries. The realistic case is between 1.87--2.30A with a peak power consumption of 53.91W--66.20W during ascent phase.

Disregarding duty cycles on components such as heaters, coolers, vacuum pumps and compressors, the average current draw is 0.66A for the duration of the flight (assuming 7.5h) and a total power consumption of 143.51Wh. However, this is not a realistic estimate due to the fact that the compressor is bottlenecked by the vacuum pumps and thus cannot run at 100\% capacity. Additionally, the heaters and coolers are very likely not needed to run on 100\% duty cycle, or even close to 100\%, to achieve optimal temperature. Because of this, estimations of the duty cycles have been added to listed components, and the numbers require further testing.

The main MCU runs on very low power, roughly 175mA at 3.3V, and the other MCUs, for example pressure MCU and thermal MCU, run on low power as well. \section{Software design}

The on-board software is responsible for collecting sensor data, storing it on the on-board SD card, handling communication with ground, and controlling the pressure and thermal subsystems. The flight implementation is planned in C++, chosen for deterministic timing, low memory overhead, and direct hardware-level control with interrupt support.

The software operates in two top-level modes: Manual and Autonomous. Autonomous operation is structured into Standby, Measurements, and Humidity loops. A Test Loop can be entered through manual command for safe diagnostics while keeping essential services active (precharge, watchdog, and telemetry heartbeat).

In manual operation, commands are processed via interrupt-driven command handling. Overrides are applied to selected subsystems for testing and troubleshooting. If connection is lost, all manual overrides are reset and the controller returns to autonomous behavior.

When communication is lost, ambient pressure is used to select fallback behavior: above 40 mbar, Measurements mode is entered; below 40 mbar, Standby mode is entered. If the lost-link condition persists and mission-end conditions are detected, termination logic is triggered.

\subsection{Main MCU}

The Main (Master) \gls{mcu} coordinates mode transitions, command handling, telemetry preparation, and SD-card scheduling. Internal and ambient pressure/temperature/humidity values are collected, validated, and forwarded to the thermal and pressure control paths.

To guarantee operation, a watchdog heartbeat is continuously maintained. Missing heartbeats trigger safe restart behavior. Data transfer between software components is organized with queue-based producer-consumer patterns to decouple sensor reads, control actions, E-Link transfer, and SD writes.

To reduce unsafe reactions from corrupted data, plausibility filtering is applied to critical measurements before control actions are executed.

\subsection{Thermal MCU}
The Thermal MCU receives data from the Main MCU and controls chamber heating, intake-air conditioning, and protection of temperature-sensitive components. If no valid thermal response is received, a restart loop is attempted; repeated failures are reported to ground with status information.

Humidity handling is implemented as a dedicated protective loop. If ambient humidity exceeds 80\% RH, shutters are kept closed and pump actions are restricted. Internal reheating is applied as needed, and loop exit is permitted only after internal humidity and temperature return to acceptable conditions. After recovery, control returns to Standby or Measurements based on ambient pressure.

Heating of intake air and heating of electronics may be run in parallel when power limits permit.

The thermal control system will use PID control logic to regulate temperature in designated areas. The control logic will run multiple parallel loops on the same MCU, each producing a duty cycle applied to PWM signals running heaters. The software will receive sensor readings and target temperatures from the main MCU via I2C communication. Within each loop the control error will be calculated once per cycle. The control error will be preserved when entering the next cycle so the derivative term can be calculated by subtracting the new error from the previous. The integral term will be updated by adding the new control error in each cycle. Anti windup will be implemented by limiting how large the integral term can get. The coefficients Kp, Ki and Kd will be programmed as constant variables and will be determined after the system is tuned. The PID control loops will be able to be terminated by command from the main MCU in the event of a failure.

\subsection{Pressure MCU}

The Pressure MCU regulates vacuum pumps and compressor to maintain chamber pressure near 3 bar. Normal control is applied in the nominal pressure band. If pressure approaches safety limits, protective actions are executed.

In overpressure conditions, shutters are opened and pumping is reduced or stopped. In underpressure conditions, pressurization restart logic is executed. If pressure cannot be recovered safely, emergency stop behavior is triggered and the fault is reported to ground.
\subsection{Interfaces}
All transmissions are saved to the on-board storage with timestamps. Similarly, all saved data is saved with timestamps, too. A table detailing the expected data sizes of data to be stored is given in Table \ref{tab:data_rates}. In total 39856 bits per second (40 kbit) will be stored. This means that for the duration of 6 hours a storage of 108 MB (megabyte) is required to store all the data received from the sensors. The SD cards onboard are more than enough for this task. Some of this data will be downlinked every second for redundancy and tracking of the functionality of the experiment. Based on the sampling rates in Table \ref{tab:data_rates}, the Elink thread can pull 100 datapoints from the telemetry and data queue to downlink each second, resulting in an estimated downlink rate of 32.8 kbps, below the experiment requirement (P.21) of 100 kbps. Assuming a maximum connection dropout duration of 10 minutes, this would likewise imply a build-up of data of 19.68 Mbit. Transferring it over the next 5 minutes gives a data rate of 65.6 kbps which is feasible. With compression it is probable that only half the rates stated need to be transmitted, but a higher data rate could be used to send duplicates or checksums for error mitigation.

The frequencies are chosen based on the datasheets of the sensors. The SHT45 sensor has multiple modes, fast mode plus (FM+) was chosen for this mission. The K96 sensor relies on the speed of the NDIR sensor within with typically is below 10 Hz, whereas in the MS5607 sensor OSR 4096 mode was chosen. The \gls{rtc} frequency was calculated to be equal to all the frequencies added. This is to consider the scenario where none of the sensors data readings collide and all the readings from the 3 sensors are collected at different timestamps, which will require one timestamp for each.

The K96 sensor core requires a UART interface and relies on the Modbus protocol, while most other sensors use I2C. The communication protocol will be UDP.

\subsection{Manual operation procedures}
Although the on-board software shall be equipped to handle exceptions from nominal conditions, an operation plan for exceptional situations is helpful. 

\paragraph{Exceptional pressure readings in the pressure chamber} are handled by the on-board software by closing the outlet valve and stopping the pumps. \dos{ref pump action schematic}
After nominal pressure is restored, the pressurization system will return to normal operations. 
However, if exceptional pressures are occurring repeatedly, a switch to manual mode might be necessary. 
Then, the team can try to identify the source of the problem.
Reasons could be a faulty sensor inside the pressure chamber, a partially frozen outlet, a software error or a faulty pump.\par 

If a pump is not working nominally, the pressure sensors measuring the pressure in the tubing between pumps should show signs of that, too. 
If a pump is not pressurizing air as much as it should, its intake may be blocked. 
The team may decide to heat up the experiment in an attempt to melt any ice.
This may be especially useful for the first vacuum pump, which is connected to the ambient air.
If the problem can not be solved, the experiment may be aborted due to unknown risks of the faulty equipment.\par 

If a software error is responsible for the exceptional pressure readings, the \gls{pid} algorithm may be misbehaving.
A restart of the pressure \gls{mcu} may be triggered in an attempt to resolve the issue. 
If an analysis of the software logs \dos{are we having any?} shows that the autonomous loop on-board the main \gls{mcu} is misbehaving, a restart of the main \gls{mcu} may be triggered.
If the problem remains unsolved, measurements may be taken in manual mode.\par 

If a partially frozen outlet is responsible for the exceptional pressure readings, the outflow hole heaters may be activated.
If the problem persists, the outlet is likely not the cause.\par 

If a faulty sensor inside the pressure chamber is responsible for the exceptional pressure readings, the collected data can not be used for meaningful scientific results.
As it may also be a security hazard, the experiment may be aborted.\par 

\paragraph{Sensor deaths} are not handled by the onboard software in the current design stages.
If a temperature sensor of a vacuum pump dies, the temperature sensor on the other vacuum pump may be used as a proxy. 
If both die or the temperature sensor on the compressor dies, the experiment may be aborted due to security risks.\newline 
If a pressure sensor inside the tubing connecting the pumps dies, the \gls{pid} algorithm might fail. 
In that case, the pumps may be controlled manually based on prior testing and control using the remaining sensors.\newline
If a pressure sensor next to a heater dies, that heater cannot be safely used anymore. It can also no longer be controlled by the \gls{pid} algorithm. 
The outlet hole heater is an exception, as it may then be used manually if the outlet is suspected to be blocked.

\section{Ground support equipment}

A Python-based \gls{gui} will be developed for ground operations during the mission. The interface will be implemented using a suitable graphical library such as Tkinter, PyQt, or PySide, depending on the final balance between simplicity, responsiveness, and development effort. For plotting and visualizing received downlink data, libraries such as Matplotlib or PyQtGraph can be used, while the downlink data itself will also be stored locally on the ground computer for later inspection and post-flight analysis.

Downlink reception will be handled by a dedicated Python process that listens to the ground station input stream, receives the incoming telemetry packets, and forwards them to the \gls{gui}. The received packets will first be decoded and validated so that malformed or incomplete messages can be filtered out before they are shown to the operator. After parsing, the values will be timestamped on the ground computer and written to local storage in a structured format such as CSV or JSON, allowing the team to preserve a full mission log even if the visual interface is closed or restarted.

The same data stream will then be used for live plots in the \gls{gui}, so that the operator can monitor pressure, temperature, humidity, and system status in real time as the packets arrive. This makes it possible to compare the most recent telemetry with previous points in the log and quickly identify anomalies during the mission. If the downlink is interrupted, the locally stored packet history will still be available for later review and recovery of the measurement timeline.

The \gls{gui} will include clickable buttons for common mission commands, allowing operators to send predefined instructions through uplink without manually typing them each time. In addition, the interface will contain a terminal section for commands or configuration options that are not part of the predefined buttons, providing flexibility for debugging, manual intervention, and ad-hoc control during operations.
